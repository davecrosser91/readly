Chapter 1

The ferry to Aldersey ran twice a day, and Nora Quinn had missed both of them. She stood at the end of the pier, watching the wake of the evening boat dissolve into the grey water, and wondered whether the universe was trying to tell her something.

"You'll be wanting Emrys," said a voice behind her. It belonged to a stout woman in a yellow raincoat who was coiling a rope with practised ease. "He takes the mail across at dusk. He'll grumble, mind you, but he never says no."

Emrys turned out to be a wiry man of indeterminate age with a boat that seemed held together by paint and stubbornness. He squinted at Nora's suitcase, then at her city shoes, and sighed with his whole body.

"Aldersey, is it? Nobody goes to Aldersey in November. Even the birds think twice."

"I've inherited a house there," Nora said. "Hollow Cottage. Do you know it?"

The change in his face was subtle but unmistakable, like a cloud crossing the sun. He busied himself with the mooring line before he answered.

"Everyone knows Hollow Cottage," he said. "Get in, then. Tide won't wait for either of us."

The crossing took forty minutes. Emrys spoke exactly once, halfway over, when the lights of the island first flickered through the dusk. "Whatever you find in that house," he said, not looking at her, "remember that the dead only keep what we refuse to take back."

Chapter 2

Hollow Cottage crouched at the edge of the village, its windows dark, its garden surrendered to brambles. Nora's great-aunt Ivy had lived there for sixty years and had left it to a niece she had never once met.

The key turned smoothly, which surprised her. Inside, the air smelled of lavender and cold ash. Nothing was covered, nothing packed away; it was as if Ivy had simply stepped out to the shop. A teacup sat on the kitchen table, washed and upturned on its saucer. Beside it lay an envelope with a single word on it: Finally.

Nora's hands were not quite steady as she opened it.

"If you are reading this, you came. That is more than the rest of them ever did, and it means the house has chosen correctly. There is a box under the floorboard beneath the west window. Your grandmother and I quarrelled over what it holds, and the quarrel outlived us both. I am trusting you to end it. Whatever you decide, decide it kindly. Kindness was the one thing we ran out of."

The floorboard lifted easily, as if it had been waiting. The box was small, tin, painted with faded roses. Inside lay a bundle of letters tied with string, a photograph of two young women laughing on the pier, and a ring with a green stone that caught the lamplight like a held breath.

Nora sat back on her heels, listening to the wind worry the eaves of the house that was now hers, and began to read the first letter.
